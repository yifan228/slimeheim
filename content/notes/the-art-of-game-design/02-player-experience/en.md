## What Is a Venue?

A venue is where an activity takes place — the actual space where players play a game. Different venues shape vastly different gaming experiences.

## Private Venues

### The Hearth

A space for shared enjoyment, where even spectators can join in the fun. Games played here tend to be performative and interactive.

> Examples: Wii, Just Dance

### The Workbench

A "leaning forward" space — a private area dedicated to tackling difficult problems. Guests rarely enter these places, like a desk or workshop. Games here tend to be more challenging, demanding focus and deep engagement.

> Examples: many strategy and puzzle games on Steam

### The Reading Nook

A "leaning back" space — typically a couch in the bedroom. Games played here are usually simple, casual, and relaxing.

## Public Venues

### The Theater & The Arena

Open, public spaces with an audience, where the game itself becomes a kind of performance.

> Examples: Twitch, YouTube live streams

## Semi-Public Venues

### The Gaming Table

A social space between public and private, like a pool table at a bar or a card table at a casino. In the game world, this maps to tabletop gaming spaces. Digital games rarely occupy this territory.

### The Playground

Spaces like a backyard, a park, or a patch of forest — the domain of children, where adults rarely play anymore. We can't exactly move a digital game into the woods, but perhaps we can bring that sense of space into a virtual world.

## Anywhere & Everywhere

For some games, the venue doesn't matter. They can be picked up and put down at any moment.

> Examples: Sudoku, crossword puzzles

## Thinking About Game Design Through the Lens of Venue

- What characteristics of this venue affect how people play?
- Which design choices in my game serve the qualities of this venue?
